## 1.3 Course Syllabus and Grading Policy

**LMS Element Type:** Static Page  
**Title:** Course Syllabus and Assessment Policy

**Content:**

```
COURSE SYLLABUS: QUALITY AND PATIENT SAFETY IN LOW-RESOURCE HEALTH SETTINGS

COURSE DESCRIPTION

This course equips health workers and managers in low-resource settings to systematically 
improve quality of care and patient safety. Learners will explore frameworks from the 
Institute for Healthcare Improvement (IHI) and the World Health Organization (WHO) and 
apply them to Nigerian health facilities. Through case studies, interactive exercises, 
and a capstone quality improvement project, participants will learn to identify safety 
problems, measure them, design solutions, and embed improvement into routine work.

LEARNING OUTCOMES

By the end of this course, learners will be able to:

1. Define quality, patient safety, adverse events, and safety culture, and describe 
   major safety challenges in Nigerian health facilities.

2. Apply the IHI Model for Improvement (aim, measures, change ideas, PDSA cycles) 
   to common patient safety problems in low-resource settings.

3. Use basic QI tools such as process mapping, root cause analysis, and run charts 
   to understand and improve care processes.

4. Design and implement simple incident reporting and learning systems that promote 
   a just culture rather than blame.

5. Select, track, and interpret a small set of safety and quality indicators 
   appropriate to their context.

6. Develop a realistic QI/patient safety project proposal addressing documented 
   safety gaps in Nigerian or similar low-resource settings.

ASSESSMENT AND GRADING

Your final grade is calculated as follows:

- Weekly Quizzes (Weeks 1, 3, 5): 15%
  * Three short quizzes (10 MCQs each, 10 points each)
  * Total: 30 points → 15% of final grade

- Weekly Applied Assignments (Weeks 1-8): 35%
  * Eight assignments varying in length and complexity
  * Total: 100 points → 35% of final grade
  * Examples: incident reflection, process map, run chart, bundle design

- Capstone Project Charter (Week 9): 25%
  * Single comprehensive assignment (3-5 pages)
  * Total: 35 points → 25% of final grade
  * Includes background, problem statement, aim, measures, change ideas, 
    governance plan, timeline

- Final Presentation + Reflection (Week 10): 25%
  * Presentation: 8-10 minutes (video or slides with audio)
  * Reflection: 400-500 word essay
  * Total: 30 points + 15 points → 25% of final grade

PASSING GRADE

- Minimum overall score: 70%
- Must submit capstone project to be eligible for micro-credential
- If you score below 70%, you may retake the course after 30 days

GRADING SCALE

Grade Range: Points → Percentage → Letter Grade
- 90-100 pts: 90-100% → A (Excellent)
- 80-89 pts: 80-89% → B (Good)
- 70-79 pts: 70-79% → C (Satisfactory)
- Below 70 pts: Below 70% → Does Not Pass

SUBMISSION DEADLINES

- Week 9 capstone: Due by end of Wednesday (23:59 UTC)
- Week 10 presentation + reflection: Due by end of Friday (23:59 UTC)

Note: Facilitator will announce specific dates on course start date.

LATE SUBMISSION POLICY

- Submissions up to 48 hours late: -10% penalty
- Submissions 48+ hours late: Not accepted without prior approval
- To request extension: Email facilitator with reason 24 hours before deadline

DISCUSSION FORUM ETIQUETTE

- Be respectful of different viewpoints and backgrounds
- Use professional language
- Cite sources if referencing others' work
- Keep posts focused on course topics
- Do not share patient identifiable information (use de-identified cases only)

ACADEMIC INTEGRITY

All work submitted must be your own. Do not:
- Copy text directly from readings or other sources without quotation and citation

Academic integrity violations will result in a failing grade for the assignment 
and may result in removal from the course.

MICRO-CREDENTIAL REQUIREMENTS

Upon successful completion (70%+ overall grade, capstone submitted), learners receive:
```

```
GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS (Embedded in course and referenced throughout)

Adverse Event: An injury caused by medical management rather than the underlying condition
Error: A failure to complete a planned action or use of a wrong plan
Near Miss: An error that did not cause patient harm
Patient Safety: Freedom from accidental injury; ensuring care is as safe as possible
Quality of Care: The degree to which health services increase the likelihood of desired outcomes
Quality Improvement (QI): Systematic approach to making health services better
Safety Culture: Shared values and behaviors that prioritize patient safety
System: Interconnected set of elements working together toward a goal
PDSA Cycle: Plan-Do-Study-Act cycle for testing improvement ideas
Run Chart: Graph showing performance over time

Students are encouraged to access WHO and IHI materials through institutional accounts
```

```
[SPEAKER: Friendly, professional tone]

"Hello, and welcome to Week 1 of Quality and Patient Safety in Low-Resource Settings.

Let me start with a story. 

A woman arrives at a primary health center pregnant and with chest pain. 
She's triaged by a junior health worker who doesn't have a protocol for 
chest pain in pregnancy. She waits 3 hours to see a nurse. By the time 
she's examined, her blood pressure is dangerously high. She's given a 
medication that's contraindicated in pregnancy because the label is faded 
and hard to read. She has a bad reaction.

Now, ask yourself: Was this a mistake? Yes. Was it just about the health worker? 
No. There was no protocol. There was no training. The equipment was broken. 
The medications were poorly stored.

This is why we talk about systems, not just individual errors.

Let me define a few key terms that will be important throughout this course.

First: What is quality of care?

Quality is the degree to which health services increase the likelihood of 
desired outcomes—better health, patient satisfaction, and cost-effectiveness. 
It's not just about following rules. It's about results.

Second: What is patient safety?

Patient safety means freedom from accidental injury. It means the care itself 
doesn't harm the patient. In low-resource settings, this is a huge challenge 
because you don't have all the resources, but you can still be systematic about it.

Now, in healthcare, we distinguish between a few types of problems:

An ERROR is when something goes wrong in the process. A prescriber writes the 
wrong dose. A nurse miscounts a syringe. An administrator forgets to order supplies. 
Errors happen in every system.

A NEAR MISS is when an error happens, but it doesn't reach the patient, or it does 
but no harm occurs. For example: A nurse picks up the wrong vial, but catches it 
before injecting. That's a near miss. Near misses are goldmines for learning, 
because they show you where your system is weak.

An ADVERSE EVENT is when an error reaches the patient and causes harm. 
The woman in my story had an adverse event.

Here's the critical insight: Most adverse events don't happen because of one person's 
mistake. They happen because of a chain of failures. The broken chain is called the 
Swiss cheese model. Each layer of the system is like a slice of cheese with holes. 
Most of the time, the holes don't line up. But sometimes they do, and that's when 
an error reaches a patient.

So when we improve patient safety, we don't just blame the individual. 
We fix the system.

In Nigeria, what are the big patient safety challenges? Research shows:

First: Medication errors. Labels are often faded or unclear. Vials are stored 
together that look alike. There's no real pharmacy system in many facilities.

Second: Infection control. Hand hygiene practices are weak. Supplies for PPE 
are limited. People reuse gloves or syringes.

Third: Communication and handoffs. When a patient moves from one place to another, 
information gets lost. A nurse doesn't tell the doctor something critical.

Fourth: Documentation. Medical records are incomplete or missing. You don't know 
what happened to the patient last time.

Fifth: Protocols and training. Many facilities don't have written protocols. 
Staff haven't been trained on how to handle common emergencies.

These aren't failures of individual people. They're failures of systems. 
And that's good news, because systems can be improved.

Throughout this course, we're going to learn how to identify these system weaknesses, 
measure them, test improvements, and make them stick.

The tools come from two organizations: The World Health Organization, which developed 
a patient safety curriculum guide that's used globally, and the Institute for 
Healthcare Improvement, which developed a framework called the Model for Improvement 
that's proven to work even in low-resource settings.

We're going to adapt these tools to Nigeria. And by the end of 10 weeks, you're going 
to design a quality improvement project that you can actually do in your facility.

So here's my challenge to you: By the end of Week 1, I want you to think about one 
safety problem in your setting. Not a blame-the-worker problem, but a systems problem. 
Something where the system is set up in a way that makes error more likely.

That problem might become your capstone project.

Let's get started.
```

```
Topic: Definition of Adverse Event

A patient receives an injection of 10 mL of a medication instead of the prescribed 
5 mL. The medication is safe at both doses, but the patient experiences temporary 
nausea that resolves within 2 hours. This event is best classified as:

A) An error only (no adverse event occurred)
B) A near miss
C) An adverse event (the injection caused temporary harm)
D) A system failure

ANSWER: C

Explanation: An adverse event is an injury caused by medical management (not the 
underlying disease). Even though the harm was temporary, the medication error caused 
patient harm, making it an adverse event. We use this term to trigger a learning 
response and system improvement.

---

Question 3 of 10 [1 point]
Topic: Definition of Near Miss

A nurse picks up a vial of adrenaline from the shelf intending to prepare an emergency

A) An adverse event
B) A medication error
C) A near miss
D) A system weakness

ANSWER: C

Explanation: A near miss is an error that doesn't reach the patient or reaches but 
causes no harm. In this case, the nurse caught the error before administration. 
Near misses are valuable for learning because they show us where systems are vulnerable.

D) Because patients don't follow instructions

ANSWER: C

Explanation: The "Swiss cheese model" shows that errors usually involve multiple
```

## 2.1 Lecture Video

**LMS Element Type:** Video (embedded)  
**Title:** Lecture 2.1 - Systems Thinking and Safety Culture  
**Duration:** 12 minutes  

**Video script:**

```
[SPEAKER: Professional, engaging tone]

"Welcome back to Week 2. I'm excited about this week because it's where the course 
really starts to click. Once you understand systems thinking, you see everything 
differently.

Last week, we talked about the woman with chest pain in pregnancy who got the wrong 
medication. I asked you: Was this her fault or the nurse's fault? And the answer is: 
it wasn't really anyone's fault. The system was set up for failure.

Let me explain systems thinking.

A system is an interconnected set of elements that work together to produce an outcome. 
In healthcare, the elements include: people (staff), processes (how we do things), 
equipment, policies, communication, and physical environment.

These elements interact. Change one, and you change the others.

Example: You implement a new hand hygiene protocol. But you don't provide hand rub. 
People revert to not washing hands. You changed the policy but not the supplies. 
The system didn't shift.

Another example: You train nurses on safe injection practices. But you don't address 
staffing levels. The PHC is so short-staffed that nurses rush. They take shortcuts. 
Training alone doesn't work if the system doesn't support the behavior.

This is why blaming individuals doesn't work. It's not that people don't care. 
It's that the system makes it hard to do the right thing.

Now, let's talk about safety culture. What is it?

Safety culture is the shared values, beliefs, and behaviors in an organization about 
what is important and how things are done regarding safety. In a healthy safety culture:

First: People feel safe reporting errors and near misses. They know they won't be 
punished for reporting. This is called a "just culture."

Second: Leadership listens. When someone reports an error, the response is investigation, 
not punishment. We ask: What happened? Why did it happen? How do we fix the system?

Third: Learning is systematic. Errors are analyzed. Improvements are tested. 
Progress is tracked.

Fourth: Responsibility is shared. Everyone is responsible for safety, from the 
cleaner to the director.

Now, contrast that with what happens in many facilities, including in Nigeria:

When an adverse event happens, people hide it. They fear punishment. There's a blame 
culture. Someone gets fired or transferred. But nothing changes in the system, so 
the same mistake happens again.

Or, errors are called "negligence" when really the system failed. We blame the 
individual instead of fixing the process.

The WHO calls this "just culture." It means we differentiate between:

One: An error that happened because the system set people up for failure. 
Response: Fix the system.

Two: A mistake that was unintentional but happened due to lack of knowledge. 
Response: Train.

Three: Negligence or willful disregard. 
Response: Disciplinary action (rare).

But the key is: Most adverse events fall into category one or two. Not category three. 
Yet we often treat them as three.

In Nigerian settings, barriers to safety culture include:

First: Fear of consequences. Nurses fear they'll lose their job if they report an error.

Second: Hierarchy. Junior staff don't feel empowered to speak up to senior doctors.

Third: Lack of time. Facilities are so busy that there's no time for safety discussions.

Fourth: Lack of resources. People think, "What's the point of reporting if we can't fix it anyway?"

Fifth: Lack of leadership commitment. If the director doesn't prioritize safety, 
nobody else will.

Sixth: Limited training. Staff haven't learned about safety culture or how to respond 
to errors.

But here's the good news: Leaders can change culture. When a director says, "We're 
going to investigate errors without blame and fix systems," it spreads. People start 
reporting. Improvement happens.

This is why quality improvement projects must have leadership support. The director, 
the head nurse, the senior clinician—they have to believe in it and model it.

By the end of this course, your capstone project will include a governance and 
leadership plan. That's not bureaucratic. That's the foundation for change.

Okay, so here's your challenge for Week 2: Look at your own setting. 
Do you see a just culture or a blame culture? What would need to change?

That reflection is Assignment 2.
```

# APPENDIX C: Case Study Scenarios for Week 6

**Case Scenario for Root Cause Analysis Assignment (Week 6)**

```
CASE SCENARIO: MEDICATION ERROR AT DISTRICT HOSPITAL

Setting: A 200-bed district hospital in Nigeria serving a population of ~500,000

Background:
The hospital has one main pharmacy, staffed by 1 pharmacist and 2 pharmacy technicians. 
The hospital also has a small satellite pharmacy on the maternal ward. Clinical wards 
store commonly used medications at the bedside.

The Incident:
At 14:30 on a Tuesday, a patient on the male medical ward (Mr. K, 58 years old) 
received an injection of 500 mg of adrenaline IV instead of 5 mg IV for acute heart failure. 
The intended medication was dobutamine 5 mg IV.

Outcome:
The patient experienced severe tachycardia, hypertension, and chest pain for ~20 minutes. 
He recovered without permanent harm, but the event was frightening for the patient and family.

Timeline of Events:
- 14:00 - Junior doctor writes prescription for "Dobutamine 5 mg IV" on patient chart
- 14:15 - Nurse goes to bedside medication store to find the drug
- 14:20 - Nurse finds vial labeled "Adrenaline 1:1000" (which is 1 mg/mL)
- 14:25 - Nurse prepares injection; dose calculation: "1 mL = 1 mg. Patient needs 5 mg, 
  so 5 mL" (This is 5 mg adrenaline, not 5 mg dobutamine)
- 14:30 - Injection administered

Details from Investigation:
1. The pharmacy had NOT stocked dobutamine on the ward; it was out of stock
2. Adrenaline and dobutamine vials are similar in appearance (both small brown bottles)
3. The vial labels were in small print and faded
4. The nurse was covering for a colleague who was on leave; she was unfamiliar 
   with the ward medication system
5. The ward had no pharmacist on duty to verify the substitution
6. There was no protocol for when medications are out of stock
7. The prescriber did not communicate directly with the nurse about what medication was prescribed
8. The patient was in a busy morning ward round; there was no time for double-checking

Questions for Case Analysis:
1. What is the primary problem?
2. Using 5 Whys, trace the root causes.
3. Create a fishbone diagram identifying system factors.
4. Propose 3-5 system improvements (not just "retrain the nurse").

FACILITATION NOTES:

This case reflects real patient safety challenges in Nigerian hospitals:
- Stock management issues (out-of-stock medications)
- Similar-looking vials without clear differentiation
- Communication breakdown (prescription not communicated to nurse)
- Lack of verification step (pharmacist verification missing)
- Staffing and fatigue (covering staff less familiar with system)
- High workload and pace (no time for double-checking)
- Lack of protocol for medication substitution

Expected learning:
Learners should identify system factors, not just individual error. 
A good answer will propose improvements like:
- Automated dispensing system or locked cabinet (reduces wrong vial selection)
- Clearer labeling (high-contrast, larger text)
- Pharmacist verification protocol
- Direct communication between prescriber and nurse
- Protocol for medication unavailability
- Safer medication substitution procedures
- Orientation protocol for covering staff
```
